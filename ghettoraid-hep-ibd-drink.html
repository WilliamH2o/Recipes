GhettoRaid HEP & IBD Drink

A homemade oral rehydration drink formulated for heat exhaustion prevention, hangovers, and IBD flares using Walmart ingredients.

Low-FODMAP Low-Oxalate

Ingredients

  • 1 liter cold water
  • 4–5 Tbsp citrus juice (lemon or lime)
  • ¼ cup hot water (for dissolving dry ingredients)
  • 1.5–2 Tbsp NOW Foods Dextrose OR 2–3 Tbsp granulated sugar (sucrose — see Notes before choosing)
  • ⅓ tsp table salt (½ tsp for heavy sweaters)
  • ⅛ tsp NoSalt (potassium chloride)

Instructions

  1. Combine the sugar, salt, and NoSalt in the hot water. Stir or shake until fully dissolved.
  2. Add the cold water and citrus juice. Shake or stir well.
  3. Refrigerate until cold before drinking.

When to Go to the ER Instead

Look. GhettoRaid is a drink, not a hospital. If any of the following apply, stop and go to the emergency room. I know, I know, it's expensive, and the chairs are bad, and someone in the waiting room is definitely going to cough on you. Go anyway.

  • You cannot keep anything down? Go.
  • You haven't peed in hours? Go.
  • Standing up makes the room spin? Go.
  • Your heart is racing at rest? Go.
  • Blood in your stool or vomit? Go.
  • Fever above 102°F with gastrointestinal symptoms? Go.
  • High-volume fluid loss lasting more than a day? Go.

This drink works when your gut can absorb faster than you're losing. The moment that math stops working, you need IV fluids. A needle in your arm. Liquid IV is a marketing lie. This is not a controversial position.

Notes

Why Sugar Is in the Drink

The sugar is not here for calories. It is here for the SGLT1 cotransporter. Glucose and sodium are absorbed together in the intestine, which means glucose accelerates sodium absorption. The WHO Oral Rehydration Solution, designed for severe dehydration from diarrheal disease, uses about 13.5g of glucose per liter. Sucrose splits into roughly 50% glucose and 50% fructose, so the effective glucose dose in this recipe is approximately 12–18g/L.

IBD and Active Flares: Use Dextrose, Not Sucrose

Use dextrose powder or finely crushed glucose tablets if your gut is inflamed. During a Crohn's flare, a UC flare, a post-gastroenteritis gut, or in people with congenital sucrase-isomaltase deficiency (more common than diagnosed), brush border enzyme activity drops. This means sucrose cannot be split efficiently, so you lose the glucose advantage. The fructose half of sucrose also does not ride SGLT1 — it uses GLUT5, and excess fructose is a known FODMAP that can worsen osmotic diarrhea. In an active flare, sucrose is the worst-case scenario for absorption and symptoms. Use dextrose.

Why It Works for Hangovers

Alcohol suppresses hepatic gluconeogenesis. The liver is busy processing ethanol and acetaldehyde and cannot maintain blood glucose at its normal baseline. A significant portion of hangover misery — the shakiness, fatigue, and cognitive fog — is mild hypoglycemia sitting underneath the dehydration. The sugar in GhettoRaid is doing SGLT1 cotransport work and blood glucose correction at the same time.

What the Citrus Is Actually Doing

For heat exhaustion and hangovers, the citrus is doing flavor work and a small amount of vitamin C work. It is not load-bearing. You could skip it, and the drink would still function. For diarrhea and IBD, the citrate matters more: it helps correct the metabolic acidosis that accompanies diarrheal fluid loss. The WHO ORS includes citrate at 10 mmol/L specifically for that reason.

Not Medical Advice

This is a recipe, not a prescription. Nothing here replaces a doctor, a pharmacist, or a medical professional who has actually examined you. If you have kidney disease, heart failure, hypertension, or any condition that requires you to manage fluid or electrolyte intake carefully, talk to someone with a medical license before drinking anything designed to shift your sodium balance. The same goes for medications that affect kidney function or electrolyte handling — diuretics, ACE inhibitors, NSAIDs, lithium, and others can interact badly with electrolyte loading.
